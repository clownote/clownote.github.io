Notes of Andrew Ng’s Machine Learning —— (8) Neural Networks: Representation

Model Representation

Let’s examine how we will represent a hypothesis function using neural networks.

At a very simple level, neurons are basically computational units that take inputs (dendrites) as electrical inputs (called “spikes”) that are channeled to outputs (axons).

In our model, our dendrites are like the input features x1⋯xnx_1\cdots x_nx1​⋯xn​, and the output is the result of our hypothesis function.

In this model our x0x_0x0​ input node is sometimes called the “bias unit.” It is always equal to 1.

In neural networks, we use the same logistic function as in classification, 11+e−θTx\frac{1}{1+e^{-\theta^Tx}}1+e−θTx1​, yet we sometimes call it a sigmoid (logistic) activation function. In this situation, our “theta” parameters are sometimes called “weights”.

Visually, a simplistic representation looks like:

[x0x1x2]→[]→hθ(x)\left[\begin{array}{c} x_0\\x_1\\x_2 \end{array}\right] \to [\quad] \to h_\theta(x) ⎣⎡​x0​x1​x2​​⎦⎤​→[]→hθ​(x)

Our input nodes (layer 1), also known as the “input layer”, go into another node (layer 2), which finally outputs the hypothesis function, known as the “output layer”. We can have intermediate layers of nodes between the input and output layers called the “hidden layers.”

In this example, we label these intermediate or “hidden” layer nodes a02⋯an2a^2_0 \cdots a^2_na02​⋯an2​ and call them “activation units.”

  • ai(j)a_i^{(j)}ai(j)​ = “activation” of unit iii in layer jjj
  • Θ(j)\Theta^{(j)}Θ(j) = matrix of weights controlling function mapping from layer jjj to layer j+1j+1j+1

If we had one hidden layer, it would look like:

[x0x1x2x3]→[a1(2)a2(2)a3(2)]→hθ(x)\left[\begin{array}{c}x_0 \\ x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3\end{array}\right]\rightarrow\left[\begin{array}{c}a_1^{(2)} \\ a_2^{(2)} \\ a_3^{(2)} \\ \end{array}\right]\rightarrow h_\theta(x) ⎣⎢⎢⎡​x0​x1​x2​x3​​⎦⎥⎥⎤​→⎣⎢⎡​a1(2)​a2(2)​a3(2)​​⎦⎥⎤​→hθ​(x)

The values for each of the “activation” nodes is obtained as follows:

a1(2)=g(Θ10(1)x0+Θ11(1)x1+Θ12(1)x2+Θ13(1)x3)a2(2)=g(Θ20(1)x0+Θ21(1)x1+Θ22(1)x2+Θ23(1)x3)a3(2)=g(Θ30(1)x0+Θ31(1)x1+Θ32(1)x2+Θ33(1)x3)hΘ(x)=a1(3)=g(Θ10(2)a0(2)+Θ11(2)a1(2)+Θ12(2)a2(2)+Θ13(2)a3(2))\begin{array}{r} a_1^{(2)} = g(\Theta_{10}^{(1)}x_0 + \Theta_{11}^{(1)}x_1 + \Theta_{12}^{(1)}x_2 + \Theta_{13}^{(1)}x_3) \\\\ a_2^{(2)} = g(\Theta_{20}^{(1)}x_0 + \Theta_{21}^{(1)}x_1 + \Theta_{22}^{(1)}x_2 + \Theta_{23}^{(1)}x_3) \\\\ a_3^{(2)} = g(\Theta_{30}^{(1)}x_0 + \Theta_{31}^{(1)}x_1 + \Theta_{32}^{(1)}x_2 + \Theta_{33}^{(1)}x_3) \\\\ h_\Theta(x) = a_1^{(3)} = g(\Theta_{10}^{(2)}a_0^{(2)} + \Theta_{11}^{(2)}a_1^{(2)} + \Theta_{12}^{(2)}a_2^{(2)} + \Theta_{13}^{(2)}a_3^{(2)}) \\\\ \end{array} a1(2)​=g(Θ10(1)​x0​+Θ11(1)​x1​+Θ12(1)​x2​+Θ13(1)​x3​)a2(2)​=g(Θ20(1)​x0​+Θ21(1)​x1​+Θ22(1)​x2​+Θ23(1)​x3​)a3(2)​=g(Θ30(1)​x0​+Θ31(1)​x1​+Θ32(1)​x2​+Θ33(1)​x3​)hΘ​(x)=a1(3)​=g(Θ10(2)​a0(2)​+Θ11(2)​a1(2)​+Θ12(2)​a2(2)​+Θ13(2)​a3(2)​)​

a1(2)=g(z1(2))a2(2)=g(z2(2))a3(2)=g(z3(2))\begin{array}{l}a_1^{(2)} = g(z_1^{(2)}) \\\\ a_2^{(2)} = g(z_2^{(2)}) \\\\ a_3^{(2)} = g(z_3^{(2)}) \\\\ \end{array} a1(2)​=g(z1(2)​)a2(2)​=g(z2(2)​)a3(2)​=g(z3(2)​)​

In other words, for layer j=2j=2j=2 and node kkk, the variable zzz will be:

zk(2)=Θk,0(1)x0+Θk,1(1)x1+⋯+Θk,n(1)xnz_k^{(2)} = \Theta_{k,0}^{(1)}x_0 + \Theta_{k,1}^{(1)}x_1 + \cdots + \Theta_{k,n}^{(1)}x_n zk(2)​=Θk,0(1)​x0​+Θk,1(1)​x1​+⋯+Θk,n(1)​xn​

The vector representation of xxx and zjz^{j}zj is:

x=[x0x1⋮xn]z(j)=[z1(j)z2(j)⋮zn(j)]x = \begin{bmatrix}x_0 \\ x_1 \\ \vdots \\ x_n\end{bmatrix} \qquad z^{(j)} = \begin{bmatrix}z_1^{(j)} \\ z_2^{(j)} \\ \vdots \\ z_n^{(j)}\end{bmatrix} x=⎣⎢⎢⎢⎡​x0​x1​⋮xn​​⎦⎥⎥⎥⎤​z(j)=⎣⎢⎢⎢⎢⎡​z1(j)​z2(j)​⋮zn(j)​​⎦⎥⎥⎥⎥⎤​

Setting x=a(1)x = a^{(1)}x=a(1), we can rewrite the equation as:

z(j)=Θ(j−1)a(j−1)z^{(j)} = \Theta^{(j-1)}a^{(j-1)} z(j)=Θ(j−1)a(j−1)

We are multiplying our matrix Θ(j−1)\Theta^{(j−1)}Θ(j−1) with dimensions sj×(n+1)s_j\times (n+1)sj​×(n+1) (where sjs_jsj​ is the number of our activation nodes) by our vector a(j−1)a^{(j-1)}a(j−1) with height (n+1)(n+1)(n+1). This gives us our vector z(j)z^{(j)}z(j) with height sjs_jsj​. Now we can get a vector of our activation nodes for layer jjj as follows:

a(j)=g(z(j))a^{(j)} = g(z^{(j)}) a(j)=g(z(j))

Where our function g can be applied element-wise to our vector z(j)z^{(j)}z(j).

We can then add a bias unit (equal to 111) to layer jjj after we have computed a(j)a^{(j)}a(j). This will be element a0(j)a_0^{(j)}a0(j)​ and will be equal to 111. To compute our final hypothesis, let’s first compute another zzz vector:

z(j+1)=Θ(j)a(j)z^{(j+1)} = \Theta^{(j)}a^{(j)} z(j+1)=Θ(j)a(j)

We get this final zzz vector by multiplying the next theta matrix after Θ(j−1)\Theta^{(j−1)}Θ(j−1) with the values of all the activation nodes we just got. This last theta matrix Θ(j)\Theta^{(j)}Θ(j) will have only one row which is multiplied by one column a(j)a^{(j)}a(j) so that our result is a single number. We then get our final result with:

hΘ(x)=a(j+1)=g(z(j+1))h_\Theta(x)=a^{(j+1)}=g(z^{(j+1)}) hΘ​(x)=a(j+1)=g(z(j+1))

Notice that in this last step, between layer jjj and layer j+1j+1j+1, we are doing exactly the same thing as we did in logistic regression. Adding all these intermediate layers in neural networks allows us to more elegantly produce interesting and more complex non-linear hypotheses.

Examples and Intuitions

A simple example of applying neural networks is by predicting x_1 \and x_2, which is the logical ‘and’ operator and is only true if both x1x_1x1​ and x2x_2x2​ are 111. The graph of our functions will look like:

[x0x1x2]→[g(z(2))]→hΘ(x)\begin{array}{c}\begin{bmatrix}x_0 \\ x_1 \\ x_2\end{bmatrix} \rightarrow\begin{bmatrix}g(z^{(2)})\end{bmatrix} \rightarrow h_\Theta(x)\end{array} ⎣⎡​x0​x1​x2​​⎦⎤​→[g(z(2))​]→hΘ​(x)​

Remember that x0x_0x0​ is our bias variable and is always 111. The g(z)g(z)g(z) is the following:

Let’s set our first Theta matrix as:

Θ(1)=[−302020]\Theta^{(1)}=\begin{bmatrix} -30 & 20 & 20 \end{bmatrix} Θ(1)=[−30​20​20​]

This will cause the output of our hypothesis to only be positive if both x1x_1x1​ and x2x_2x2​ are 111. In other words:

hΘ(x)=g(−30+20x1+20x2)x1=0  and  x2=0  then  g(−30)≈0x1=0  and  x2=1  then  g(−10)≈0x1=1  and  x2=0  then  g(−10)≈0x1=1  and  x2=1  then  g(10)≈1\begin{array}{ll}& h_\Theta(x) = g(-30 + 20x_1 + 20x_2) \\ \\ & x_1 = 0 \ \ and \ \ x_2 = 0 \ \ then \ \ g(-30) \approx 0 \\ & x_1 = 0 \ \ and \ \ x_2 = 1 \ \ then \ \ g(-10) \approx 0 \\ & x_1 = 1 \ \ and \ \ x_2 = 0 \ \ then \ \ g(-10) \approx 0 \\ & x_1 = 1 \ \ and \ \ x_2 = 1 \ \ then \ \ g(10) \approx 1\end{array} ​hΘ​(x)=g(−30+20x1​+20x2​)x1​=0  and  x2​=0  then  g(−30)≈0x1​=0  and  x2​=1  then  g(−10)≈0x1​=1  and  x2​=0  then  g(−10)≈0x1​=1  and  x2​=1  then  g(10)≈1​

A Neural Network for XNOR

The Θ(1)\Theta^{(1)}Θ(1) matrices for AND, NOR and OR are:

AND:Θ(1)=[−302020]NOR:Θ(1)=[10−20−20]OR:Θ(1)=[−102020]\begin{array}{l}AND:\\&\Theta^{(1)} &=\begin{bmatrix}-30 & 20 & 20\end{bmatrix} \\ NOR:\\&\Theta^{(1)} &= \begin{bmatrix}10 & -20 & -20\end{bmatrix} \\ OR:\\&\Theta^{(1)} &= \begin{bmatrix}-10 & 20 & 20\end{bmatrix} \\\end{array} AND:NOR:OR:​Θ(1)Θ(1)Θ(1)​=[−30​20​20​]=[10​−20​−20​]=[−10​20​20​]​

We can combine these to get the XNOR logical operator (which gives 111 if x1x_1x1​ and x2x_2x2​ are both 000 or both 111):

[x0x1x2]→[a1(2)a2(2)]→[a(3)]→hΘ(x)\begin{bmatrix}x_0 \\ x_1 \\ x_2\end{bmatrix} \rightarrow\begin{bmatrix}a_1^{(2)} \\ a_2^{(2)} \end{bmatrix} \rightarrow\begin{bmatrix}a^{(3)}\end{bmatrix} \rightarrow h_\Theta(x) ⎣⎡​x0​x1​x2​​⎦⎤​→[a1(2)​a2(2)​​]→[a(3)​]→hΘ​(x)

For the transition between the first and second layer, we’ll use a Θ(1)\Theta^{(1)}Θ(1) matrix that combines the values for AND and NOR:

Θ(1)=[−30202010−20−20]\Theta^{(1)} =\begin{bmatrix}-30 & 20 & 20 \\ 10 & -20 & -20\end{bmatrix} Θ(1)=[−3010​20−20​20−20​]

For the transition between the second and third layer, we’ll use a Θ(2)\Theta^{(2)}Θ(2) matrix that uses the value for OR:

Θ(2)=[−102020]\Theta^{(2)} =\begin{bmatrix}-10 & 20 & 20\end{bmatrix} Θ(2)=[−10​20​20​]

Let’s write out the values for all our nodes:

a(2)=g(Θ(1)⋅x)a(3)=g(Θ(2)⋅a(2))hΘ(x)=a(3)\begin{array}{ll}& a^{(2)} = g(\Theta^{(1)} \cdot x) \\& a^{(3)} = g(\Theta^{(2)} \cdot a^{(2)}) \\& h_\Theta(x) = a^{(3)}\end{array} ​a(2)=g(Θ(1)⋅x)a(3)=g(Θ(2)⋅a(2))hΘ​(x)=a(3)​
